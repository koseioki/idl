## Who is affected by geographical location?

Certain groups face disadvantages in the digital society and in accessing digital media due to where they live, or where they are located.

These groups include:

- Rural areas
- Remote communities
- The Global South

## Challenges

People who are in rural or remote locations often face more challenges than those in urban and densely populated areas.

These challenges include:

- Limited access to services and digital networks
- High costs of mobile and internet services
- Lower levels of digital literacy and motivation
- Lack of contextually and culturally appropriate solutions

## Causes of the challenges

Geographical location is often linked to several factors that contribute to digital inequality.

- Rural areas often have older populations
- Income levels tend to be lower in rural communities
- Education levels are generally lower
- Many jobs and local activities do not require the use of technology
- People may be less interested in or less familiar with technology

All of these factors are connected and together contribute to digital inequality.

## Why is infrastructure not well organised?

The lack of digital connectivity in rural areas is mainly due to how the telecommunications market works.

In many countries, including the United States and the EU, telecom companies have been privatised, and markets have been deregulated. This means that investment decisions are largely driven by profit.

In reality, the market is often dominated by a small number of companies, sometimes even a single provider. Building network infrastructure in rural areas is expensive because homes are far apart and populations are small.

As a result, companies cannot easily benefit from economies of scale or make a strong return on their investments.

Because of these challenges, rural areas are often seen as unprofitable, which leads to limited development of digital infrastructure.
