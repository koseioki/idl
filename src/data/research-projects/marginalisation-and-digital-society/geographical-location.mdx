Geographical location changes how people use information and communication technology (ICT). It can affect internet speed, access to devices, digital skills, and the chances people have to take part in online life. These differences shape daily life, work, education, and fairness in society.

## ICT access is not equal everywhere

In cities, people often have better internet coverage, more public Wi-Fi, and more shops that sell phones, computers, and repair services. In rural areas, internet connections can be slower or less reliable, and some places may have fewer service options. This means people in different places do not always have the same chance to use ICT in the same way.

Location also affects the price of access. In some areas, internet packages are affordable and easy to get. In other places, people may pay more for weaker service. For families with limited money, even small differences in cost can decide whether they can stay connected.

## Digital society depends on place

A digital society is a society where many services, jobs, and social activities happen online. People use ICT to study, shop, bank, work, and communicate. If someone lives in a place with strong digital infrastructure, they can usually join these activities more easily.

When a location has poor connectivity, people may be left out. A student may struggle with online classes, a worker may miss remote job opportunities, and a patient may have trouble using telehealth services. This can make life harder and increase the gap between people who are connected and people who are not.

## Social justice and the digital divide

Social justice means fairness in how people are treated and what opportunities they receive. When ICT is unevenly available across places, it creates a digital divide. The digital divide is the gap between people who have good access to technology and those who do not.

This gap is not only about internet cables or mobile signal. It is also about skills, language, disability access, and support. For example, people in remote areas may need more help using digital services, while people in low-income neighborhoods may need cheaper access and public computer spaces.

## What can help reduce inequality

Governments, schools, and companies can make a difference. They can improve broadband infrastructure, support public internet access, and make digital services easier to use. They can also offer training so people can build digital skills.

Good policy matters because equal access to ICT helps people study, work, and participate in society. When location no longer limits digital access, society becomes fairer and more inclusive.

## Simple example

Imagine two students. One lives in a city with fast internet and a laptop at home. The other lives in a remote village with weak mobile signal and no computer. The first student can join online lessons easily, while the second may fall behind even if both are equally capable. This shows how location can shape opportunity.

## Conclusion

Geographical location strongly affects ICT use, digital society, and social justice. It influences access, cost, skills, and participation. Reducing these location-based gaps is important if everyone is to have a fair chance in the digital world.
